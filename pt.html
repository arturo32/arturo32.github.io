Triangle lerp

Linear interpolation but with areas! In a triangle!

Triangle Lerp

This one I've taken from a Twitter thread by Freya Holmér. I was used to the idea of linear interpolation for the Drawing Project, but I had never heard of interpolating between three points at the time.

As Freya says in her thread, to calculate the influence of each triangle vertex in an arbitrary point you have to take the area of the inner triangle opposite to that vertex. Then calculate the percentage of that area relative to the total area of the larger triangle.

A similar approach was used in the Ray Tracer project to make surfaces appear smoother in triangle meshes (Phong Shading).

A GIF with black background showing a triangle with a circle moving in a circular motion inside it, with edges connecting it with the triangle vertices. Each vertex is in a different color: red, blue and green and each edge interpolates between these colors. The circle changes colors while moving.

Times table

Wonderful patterns emerge when numbers are written in a circle.

Times table

...times table? That's right! The GIF in the thumbnail is simply a 2 times table, where each point on the circumference is a number and each line connects a number to its double. Thus, 2 connects to 4, 3 connects to 6, and so on. In the image below we only have 12 numbers. So, from 6 and beyond we do what's called clock arithmetic, or more precisely, modular arithmetic. 0 is 12, 1 is 13, 2 is 14, and so on... This relation between the numbers is called congruence. That's why 6 connects to 0 and 7 connects to 2. We can called it the 2 time table module 12:

Julia sets

Move your mouse to generate beautiful products of a complex number function.

Julia sets

The Mandelbrot set is a set of complex numbers that look pretty when plotted in a complex plane like the one below:

To generate this is relative simple. It all boils down to the function below:

\(f_c(z) = z^2 + c\)

We will talk about the both complex numbers \(z\) and \(c\) soon. First, we need another pair of numbers:

  • A positive number greater than 2. Let's call it the threshold;
  • Another positive number. This time a larger one. This will be our number of iterations.

Now, we apply our function recursively to each pixel of an image representing the complex plane. We do this by making \(c\) the coordinate of the current pixel and \(z\) starting with the value \(0 + 0i\). As we iterate, \(z\) may or may not grow beyond our threshold. If it gets there before we reach the number of iterations, we stop the recursion and paint the pixel white. If not, we paint it black. That's it!

And Julia sets are almost the same thing, with \(c\) and \(z\) switching places, i.e., \(z\) will represent a pixel coordinate while \(c\) will be... an arbitrary constant! For each \(c\) we choose at the start of our process, we can make entirely different sets. Each of them possessing a unique complexity and beauty. And for making things even prettier we can add a little bit of math magic to the RGB values, changing them by how fast some of the inputs can reach our threshold, like the image below made with \(c = 0 - 0.8i\):

Finally, the GIF of the thumbnail of this project was made by changing \(c\) as the coordinates of the cursor. So at each new position of the mouse the function iterated thought all the pixels to render the image for that particular set. To save the processor, I made the function skip some pixels. This is why the colors are a bit washed out.

A GIF of a colorful rectangle with a black circle inside. Its colors are very pale pastels from pink to blue to yellow. As the cursor moves, the boundary of the circle morphs drastically, with opposite sides of the circle merging in a psychedelic way.

Fourier series

The Fourier series it's a way of using sine and cosine functions that can approximate other functions when summed, a bit like the Taylor series, which many learn in the Calculus course in university.

A very famous example on the internet are GIFs that show the Fourier series being used to express a square wave from sin functions. They beautifully represent the sines functions being added as circles that rotate around themselves, with different frequencies. Ray Tracer

3D ray tracer that can create photorealistic images.

Ray Tracer

This project was developed by me and my friend João Vítor throughout the course of Computer Graphics, taught by professor Selan Rodrigues dos Santos. It creates images representing 3D scenes described by XML files. All of this using the magic of ray tracing.

Ray tracing is a technique used to create 3D images where photo-realism is desired. It works by simulating photons of light as rays (vectors) that are shot at the scene from the viewer's perspective. The rays then travel through the scene being reflected, refracted or absorbed by objects. All of these interactions are calculated for each pixel of the image, defining its final color.

Doing this project was challenging because of its size. We had to take into account the camera, objects and their interactions with rays, different types of lights, different light models, parsing XML files and .obj files... At the same time it was very rewarding because at each step we could see the result of what we had done at that point.

In the end, we couldn't believe the images that our own program made! The (optional) spherical background made the reflective surfaces much more interesting!

A GIF of five images alternating: 3 colored spheres on reflective black square, a donut in space that reflects 100% of its surrounding, a mirrored Stanford dragon sitting in an open green field, two mirrored spheres floating in the same field and a tilted green Utah teapot above a square mirror reflecting the blue sky of the same field.

Som Imaginário

Educational project about sound and how humans perceive its origin in space.

Som Imaginário

This was the final project of the Embedded Systems course taught by Professor Mônica Magalhães Pereira.

It's an electronic education about the physics of sound and how humans can perceive its origin. It is intended for primary and secondary school classrooms. The device, made with Arduino, two sound sensors, an LCD display, a potentiometer and one stepper motor can indicate the origin of loud sounds, by measuring the difference in the time it takes for the sound to reach each sensor.

A GIF of five images alternating: one is a diagram of an electronic project with an arduino, two sound sensors, a lcd screen, a motor and a potentiometer.

One of the game modes involves someone clapping their hands while the device tries to point to their location. Since the device only has 2 sound sensors, it can point to the opposite location of a particular sound. This can lead to discussions about why the device behave likes that and how it knows where to point to.

I was inspired by a video from the Smarter Every Day YouTube channel, as well as a video from Steve Mould's channel.
